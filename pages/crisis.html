Safety Planning Guide

Steps you can take to protect yourself

1. Identify Safe Places

Know where you can go if you need to leave quickly: a friend's house, family member's home, shelter, public place, or library. Memorize important addresses and phone numbers.

2. Create a Signal System

Establish a code word or signal with trusted friends, family, or neighbors that means you need help immediately. This can be a text, phone call, or in-person signal.

3. Pack an Emergency Bag

Keep important documents, money, medications, clothes, and essentials in a safe place where you can grab them quickly if needed. Consider keeping a bag with a trusted friend.

4. Document Everything

Keep records of incidents, take photos of injuries, save threatening messages. Store copies in a safe place (with a trusted person or in the cloud using a secure, private account).

5. Know Your Legal Rights

Learn about protective orders, your rights as a survivor, and local laws. Many legal aid organizations offer free consultations for domestic violence cases.

6. Digital Safety

Change passwords, check privacy settings, be aware that phones/computers can be monitored. Use devices at a library or friend's house if needed. Clear browser history.

7. Plan Your Exit Route

If you need to leave, know the fastest way out of your home. Practice the route. Keep car keys and phone accessible. Consider telling neighbors to call police if they hear disturbances.

8. Financial Planning

Try to save money in a safe place. Keep important financial documents secure. Consider opening a bank account at a different bank. Know where financial resources are located.

Build Your Trusted Circle

Identify 5 trusted adults you can talk to when you need help

Having trusted adults in your life is important. These should be people who make you feel safe, listen to you, and can help in different situations. Think about family members, teachers, counselors, coaches, or family friends.
